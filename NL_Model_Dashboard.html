Hybrid Merit Order Stack Model — Netherlands 2023

Interactive dashboard for a tranche-based electricity price model calibrated against EPEX day-ahead prices using Bayesian optimisation. The model captures cross-border flows, CHP must-run dynamics, fuel cost pass-through, and weather-driven effects (solar irradiance, temperature, humidity) to evaluate storage arbitrage economics.

What does this model do?

This model simulates Dutch wholesale electricity prices by constructing a Merit Order Stack from the actual NL power plant fleet and dispatching generators against hourly residual demand (total load minus wind, solar, and net imports). Instead of treating each plant as a single price-quantity pair, we apply 9-tranche bid spreading (Ward et al. 2019) — splitting each generator's capacity into tranches that bid at different fractions of their marginal cost. This smooths the supply curve and produces more realistic price distributions, including negative prices from CHP must-run generation.

The tranche parameters are calibrated against real EPEX day-ahead prices using Bayesian optimisation (Optuna TPE, 1,000 trials) followed by Powell conjugate-direction refinement. The calibrated model is then used to evaluate how accurately storage arbitrage revenues can be estimated — the key output for investment decisions.

How is energy trading incorporated?

In the real EPEX day-ahead market, generators submit price-quantity bid pairs for each hour of the next day. The market operator (EPEX SPOT) stacks all bids from cheapest to most expensive, forming a supply curve. Where this supply curve intersects demand, the market-clearing price is set — and every accepted generator receives this price, not their own bid.

Our model replicates this mechanism. Each plant's Short-Run Marginal Cost (SRMC = fuel price ÷ efficiency + CO₂ price × emission factor ÷ efficiency) determines its base bid. But in reality, generators don't bid exactly at SRMC — they engage in strategic bidding: some tranches bid below cost to guarantee dispatch, others bid above cost to capture scarcity rents. The 9-tranche spreading models precisely this behaviour — splitting each plant's capacity into tranches bidding at 45% below to 109% above SRMC. This is why the tranche model produces a smooth, realistic supply curve instead of a sharp staircase.

Negative prices occur when CHP plants (combined heat & power) must run to supply district heating in winter, regardless of electricity prices. They bid at negative prices (down to −150 EUR/MWh) to stay dispatched. When enough CHP + wind + solar exceeds demand, the market price turns negative — a real phenomenon visible in 3.6% of 2023 hours. Cross-border flows from neighbouring countries (Germany, Belgium, Denmark, Norway, UK) are incorporated as a demand adjustment: exports increase the residual demand NL plants must serve, imports decrease it.

What are Tranches and Why Do They Matter?

In a traditional merit order model, each power plant is represented as a single price-quantity block bidding at its Short-Run Marginal Cost (SRMC). This produces a sharp “staircase” supply curve that poorly reproduces real-world price distributions. The tranche-based approach (Ward et al., 2019) addresses this by splitting each generator’s capacity into 9 tranches, each bidding at a different fraction of the plant’s SRMC.

Each tranche has two parameters: a width (what fraction of the plant’s capacity it represents) and a bid adjustment (how far above or below SRMC it bids). For example, a tranche with width 0.15 and adjustment −0.40 means 15% of the plant’s capacity bids at 60% of its marginal cost — modelling strategic underbidding to guarantee dispatch. Conversely, a tranche with adjustment +1.0 bids at double the marginal cost, capturing scarcity rents when demand is high.

The calibration of these parameters is performed through Bayesian optimisation using the Optuna library’s Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE), which intelligently samples the 38-dimensional parameter space guided by a surrogate model. This converges in 500–1,000 iterations (roughly 60–120 seconds), followed by Powell conjugate-direction refinement for fine-tuning. The objective is to minimise a weighted combination of RMSE, volatility ratio, tail accuracy, and correlation against observed EPEX day-ahead prices.

The purpose of this calibrated tranche model is to accurately reproduce the price volatility of the Dutch market — particularly negative prices driven by CHP must-run obligations and renewable oversupply — enabling a realistic assessment of storage arbitrage revenues. Standard marginal-cost models tend to compress price distributions and underestimate variability, leading to inaccurate storage valuations. By training the model on observable market conditions (fuel prices, demand, renewable generation), it can predict price drivers and capture a significant fraction (>60% targeted) of observed storage revenues while maintaining economic interpretability.

Data Sources & Inputs

Data Collection & Alignment

Nine primary data sources are loaded and aligned to hourly resolution for calendar year 2023. Plant-level generation from the ENTSO-E Transparency Platform (generation per unit, 2023–2024) is used for fleet verification and validation. Energy-Charts generation data (15-min) is resampled to hourly means. Investing.com fuel price CSVs are parsed with custom date handling. Coal prices are converted from USD/tonne to EUR/MWh using daily EUR/USD exchange rates and a thermal content of 6.978 MWh/tonne. DST duplicate hours are deduplicated. The final aligned dataset contains 8,760 hourly rows covering load, wind, solar, residual demand, gas/coal/CO₂ prices, and cross-border flows.

Merit Order Supply Curves

Supply Curve Construction

Each supply curve is built by sorting generators by their Short-Run Marginal Cost (SRMC) at a given fuel/CO₂ price snapshot. The Basic MOS is a simple staircase (one bid per plant). The Ward UK Defaults apply the 9-tranche spreading from Ward et al. (2019) with UK-calibrated parameters. The Calibrated curve uses our Bayesian-optimised parameters for the Dutch market, producing the smoothest and most realistic curve shape.

Cross-border Flow Treatment

In the model, net exports increase residual demand (more generation needed), while net imports decrease it. Calibration Results

Calibration Methodology

The model's 44 parameters are calibrated in two stages. First, Bayesian optimisation via Optuna's Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) explores the parameter space over 1,000 trials, efficiently narrowing in on promising regions. Then, Powell's conjugate-direction method (SciPy) refines the best TPE solution with up to 1,000 additional iterations, performing local gradient-free optimisation in the 34-dimensional space.

The objective function is a weighted combination of RMSE, volatility ratio, tail accuracy (P10/P90 errors), negative price frequency match, correlation, and duration curve shape. Parameters include 9 tranche widths, 9 tranche price adjustments, spreading sigma, fuel price multipliers (α), 4-band CHP must-run bids, and the price adjustment layer coefficients (γ).

Sensitivity Analysis

Parameter Sensitivity Testing

To understand model robustness, we test 28 scenarios across 6 categories: plant efficiency assumptions, fuel prices, CO₂ prices, CHP must-run capacity, cross-border flow treatment, and tranche spreading parameters. Each scenario perturbs one input while holding others at calibrated values, measuring the impact on RMSE, mean price, and other metrics. Key finding: CHP must-run capacity and gas prices are the dominant drivers; coal prices have near-zero impact because coal plants are rarely marginal in the Netherlands.

Out-of-Sample Validation — 2024

Model Generalisation: 2023 Parameters → 2024 Data

This section tests whether the model calibrated on 2023 data can accurately predict prices in 2024 — a year it has never seen. The 44 calibration parameters (tranche widths, adjustments, CHP bands, price adjustment layer, efficiency multipliers, wind interaction, scarcity adder, and weather terms) are frozen at their 2023-optimal values and applied to 2024 fuel prices, demand, wind/solar generation, and cross-border flows. If the model generalises well, the out-of-sample RMSE should be comparable to the in-sample result, validating that the calibration captures structural market dynamics rather than overfitting to 2023-specific noise.

This out-of-sample test is a key contribution to the thesis: it demonstrates that the hybrid Merit Order Stack approach produces transferable parameter estimates, not just a curve fit to one year of data.
