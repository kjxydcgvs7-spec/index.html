What does this model do?

This is a hybrid Merit Order Stack (MOS) model for the Dutch electricity market. It simulates hourly wholesale prices by constructing supply curves from 34 thermal power plants, using tranche-based bid spreading (Ward et al. 2019) to capture real-world bidding behaviour. The model includes CHP must-run dynamics, cross-border flows, and weather-driven price adjustments.

The 44 parameters were calibrated against 2023 EPEX day-ahead prices using Bayesian optimisation (Optuna TPE, 500 trials) followed by SciPy Powell refinement. The calibrated model is then used to evaluate battery storage arbitrage revenues under current and future market conditions.

Key Innovations

Tranche spreading: Each generator’s capacity is split into 9 tranches with individually calibrated bid adjustments, creating a smooth supply curve that captures strategic bidding and part-loading. This approach, adapted from Ward et al. (2019), produces more realistic price distributions than simple step-function merit orders.

Multi-band CHP must-run: The Dutch market has ~9 GW of decentral CHP with heat obligations. Rather than a single must-run bid, the model uses 4 calibrated price bands, capturing the real range of CHP bidding from negative (must-run) to positive (flexible operation).

Weather-enhanced price adjustment: Solar irradiance (proxy for 22 GW rooftop PV not in utility-scale data), temperature (gas demand competition), and humidity (overcast/still weather regime) terms capture structural price effects that generalise well to unseen data.

Data Collection & Alignment

Nine primary data sources are loaded and aligned to hourly resolution for calendar year 2023. Plant-level generation from the ENTSO-E Transparency Platform (generation per unit, 2023–2024) is used for fleet verification and validation. Energy-Charts generation data (15-min) is resampled to hourly means. Investing.com fuel price CSVs are parsed with custom date handling. Coal prices are converted from USD/tonne to EUR/MWh using daily EUR/USD exchange rates and a thermal content of 6.978 MWh/tonne. DST duplicate hours are deduplicated. The final aligned dataset contains 8,760 hourly rows covering load, wind, solar, residual demand, gas/coal/CO₂ prices, and cross-border flows.

Supply Curve Construction

Each supply curve is built by sorting generators by their Short-Run Marginal Cost (SRMC) at a given fuel/CO₂ price snapshot. The Basic MOS is a simple staircase (one bid per plant). The Ward UK Defaults apply the 9-tranche spreading from Ward et al. (2019) with UK-calibrated parameters. The Calibrated curve uses our Bayesian-optimised parameters for the Dutch market, producing the smoothest and most realistic curve shape.

Calibration Results

Calibration Methodology

The model's 44 parameters are calibrated in two stages. First, Bayesian optimisation via Optuna's Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) explores the parameter space over 1,000 trials, efficiently narrowing in on promising regions. Then, Powell's conjugate-direction method (SciPy) refines the best TPE solution with up to 1,000 additional iterations, performing local gradient-free optimisation in the 34-dimensional space.

The objective function is a weighted combination of RMSE, volatility ratio, tail accuracy (P10/P90 errors), negative price frequency match, correlation, and duration curve shape. Parameters include 9 tranche widths, 9 tranche price adjustments, spreading sigma, fuel price multipliers (α), 4-band CHP must-run bids, and the price adjustment layer coefficients (γ).

Scenarios Storage Simulation — Storage Revenue Impact

Methodology: What This Analysis Does

This analysis applies the calibrated 44-parameter model to hypothetical near-term futures (2025–2027) to assess how changes in gas prices, renewable capacity, demand, and CHP must-run obligations affect battery storage arbitrage revenues. The calibrated parameters are kept frozen — only the input data (hourly fuel prices, wind generation, demand, CHP profiles) is modified to represent different “worlds.”

This is methodologically clean: it asks “given the calibrated Dutch market structure, how does storage value change if external conditions shift?”

⚠ Model Limitations Under Crisis Conditions

Cross-border flow reversal: The model uses 2023 flow profiles (NL was net exporter, −625 MW mean). During a gas crisis, NL’s expensive gas fleet would make imports from French nuclear and German coal cheaper — NL would likely become a net importer, moderating the real price increase. This is not modelled.

Non-linear gas pass-through: The γgas coefficient (1.94) was calibrated at gas prices of 23–74 EUR/MWh. At 140–180 EUR/MWh, the linear relationship may weaken. In 2022, pass-through showed diminishing returns at extreme prices.

Regulatory intervention: The EU Market Correction Mechanism caps TTF at ~180 EUR/MWh. Above this level, emergency measures (price caps, windfall taxes, demand mandates) distort the market in ways the model cannot capture. The “Extreme” scenario is set at this cap level.

Demand destruction: Modelled as a flat multiplier on residual demand. Real demand destruction is concentrated in energy-intensive industry, is price-elastic, and time-varying — not uniform.

CHP cost squeeze: Modelled via uniform chp_mult scaling. Real CHP dynamics are seasonal (winter heat obligations vs. summer flexibility) and sector-specific (industrial vs. district heating).

How to Read These Results

Each scenario modifies the 2023 hourly input data with multipliers (e.g., gas ×2.4 means TTF rises from ~41 to ~100 EUR/MWh). The model then simulates 8,760 hourly prices and runs Li-ion storage arbitrage (1 MW / 4 MWh, 86% round-trip efficiency). All scenarios show storage revenue increases — both renewable oversupply (more negative price hours = cheap charging) and gas crises (higher volatility = wider spreads) benefit storage. This is the central finding: storage acts as a hedge against multiple future uncertainties.

Central Finding: Storage Benefits From Every Future

Across all 7 named scenarios, storage arbitrage profit increases relative to the 2023 baseline — ranging from +28% (Electrification Push) to +208% (Crisis Extreme). This makes battery storage a hedge against multiple uncertainties:

Renewables pathway: More wind and solar create frequent oversupply with negative price hours (cheap charging) and high-price calm periods (expensive discharging). The wider daily spread drives profit even as mean prices fall.

Gas crisis pathway: Higher gas prices lift the entire supply curve, increasing both price levels and volatility. Storage exploits the wider gap between cheap off-peak (renewables still generating) and expensive peak (gas-fired marginal) hours.

This dual benefit is the key policy-relevant result: regardless of whether the energy transition accelerates or a supply disruption occurs, price volatility rises — and that is exactly what storage monetises.

What Happens Inside the Model During a Gas Crisis?

Supply curve shift: Gas plants (~70% of NL dispatchable capacity) see SRMC rise proportionally. A CCGT bidding at €85/MWh at baseline would bid at ~€230/MWh under the Severe scenario. The entire right side of the merit order shifts upward.

Coal becomes competitive: Coal SRMC barely changes, so coal plants (3 GW) move left in the merit order and become baseload-competitive. However, NL has limited coal capacity.

CHP cost squeeze: CHP operators face extreme fuel costs but retain heat obligations. Some industrial CHP shuts down (reflected in chp_mult < 1.0), reducing the must-run block that creates negative prices — explaining why negative price hours decrease under gas crisis scenarios.

Wider price spreads: The combination of high base prices, volatile gas input, and maintained renewable output creates wider daily price spreads — exactly what storage arbitrage exploits.

What Happens During Renewables Saturation?

Residual demand collapse: 2× wind generation dramatically reduces residual demand during windy hours. More hours see oversupply where CHP must-run bids set negative prices.

1,943 negative price hours: Nearly 7× the baseline (280h). These provide extremely cheap charging windows for storage.

Lower mean prices but higher volatility: Mean drops from €90 to €72/MWh, but standard deviation rises from €47 to €77/MWh. Storage profits rise +93% because volatility, not level, drives arbitrage.

γwind amplifies the effect: The calibrated wind interaction term (−11.18 EUR/MWh per GW above mean) further depresses prices during high-wind hours, creating deeper charging troughs.

Scenario Total Impact NL — Comprehensive Market Analysis

About This Analysis

This tab extends the Scenarios Storage Simulation by computing full Dutch electricity market impacts across all 7 scenarios. Beyond storage arbitrage revenue, we quantify: wholesale market cost (EUR bn), generator profitability by fuel type, coal vs gas competitiveness shifts, price distribution changes (volatility, spikes, negative prices), scarcity and oversupply regimes, renewable integration metrics (wind value factor, curtailment proxy), CHP economics, and temporal price patterns. Reading These Numbers

Baseline Wholesale (9.95 EUR bn): total annual cost of electricity purchased on the wholesale market — price × demand summed over 8,760 hours. Mean Price: volume-weighted average hourly price. Negative Hours: hours when oversupply (wind + CHP must-run) pushes prices below zero — generators pay consumers to take power. Scarcity Hours: hours when residual demand exceeds 14 GW (the scarcity threshold from calibration), triggering non-linear price spikes. Gas Fleet Revenue: estimated total infra-marginal rent for all gas plants — the margin earned when market price exceeds their short-run marginal cost (SRMC). Wind Value Factor: ratio of average price during high-wind hours to overall average price — values below 1.0 indicate wind cannibalisation (wind depresses its own revenue).

⚠ Model Scope & Limitations

SRMC-only costing: The model uses Short-Run Marginal Cost (fuel ÷ efficiency + CO&sub2; ÷ efficiency). Fixed O&M costs (~15–25 EUR/kW/yr for CCGT, ~30–40 EUR/kW/yr for coal), start-up costs (5–15 EUR/MWh for OCGTs), and capital expenditure are not included. This means generator revenue figures reflect gross margins, not net profitability. Plants may show positive margins but still fail to cover fixed costs — particularly relevant for low-capacity-factor gas plants in renewable-heavy scenarios.

No CAPEX for scenario infrastructure: Scenarios like “Renewables Saturation” (wind ×2.0) and “Electrification Push” (demand ×1.15) assume new capacity and load exist, but do not account for the billions in investment required to build that wind capacity, grid reinforcement, heat pumps, or EV infrastructure. The wholesale cost figures therefore understate true system cost.

Frozen cross-border flows: All scenarios use 2023 flow profiles (NL net exporter, −625 MW mean). In reality, during a gas crisis NL’s expensive gas fleet would be undercut by French nuclear and German lignite — NL would flip to net importer, significantly moderating price spikes. Conversely, high-renewable scenarios could increase NL exports. This is the largest structural limitation for crisis scenarios.

No fleet entry/exit: The 34-plant fleet is held constant. In practice, sustained low capacity factors would trigger plant closures (reducing supply) while high prices would attract new investment (increasing supply). The model cannot capture these dynamic equilibrium effects.

Linear fuel pass-through at extremes: The γgas coefficient (1.94) was calibrated at gas prices of 23–74 EUR/MWh. At 140–180 EUR/MWh (crisis scenarios), pass-through may be non-linear. The 2022 energy crisis showed diminishing pass-through at extreme price levels due to demand destruction and regulatory intervention.

Stage 2: Merit Order Stack Construction

For each hour: compute costs → split into tranches → add CHP must-run → sort → clear against demand

1 Short-Run Marginal Cost (SRMC)

The SRMC is the variable cost of generating one additional MWh of electricity. It determines a generator's position in the merit order — cheaper plants dispatch first.

Core Formula
SRMC Calculation (per generator, per hour)
SRMC = Fuel_Price / (η × eff_mult) + CO₂_Price × CI / (η × eff_mult)

Components:
Fuel_Price = TTF gas price or API2 coal price (EUR/MWh thermal)
η = Base efficiency from fleet data (e.g. CCGT: 0.58, Coal: 0.46)
eff_mult = Calibrated efficiency multiplier per technology group:
CCGT: 1.033 (bounds ±8%) → effective η = 0.58 × 1.033 = 0.599
OCGT: 1.124 (bounds ±15%) → effective η = 0.35 × 1.124 = 0.393
Coal: 1.006 (bounds ±5%) → effective η = 0.46 × 1.006 = 0.463
CHP decentral: 0.926 (bounds ±20%)
CI = Carbon intensity (tCO₂/MWh thermal):
Gas: 0.202 | Coal: 0.341 | Lignite: 0.364 | Oil: 0.267
CO₂_Price = EU ETS carbon price (EUR/tCO₂)

Fuel cost component: Fuel_Price / (η × eff_mult) ← EUR/MWh electrical
Carbon cost component: CO₂_Price × CI / (η × eff_mult) ← EUR/MWh electrical
Worked example — Eemshaven CCGT (1,300 MW, η=0.59):
Gas price = 41.4 EUR/MWh, CO₂ = 84.6 EUR/tCO₂, eff_mult_ccgt = 1.033
Effective η = 0.59 × 1.033 = 0.609
Fuel cost = 41.4 / 0.609 = 67.9 EUR/MWh
Carbon cost = 84.6 × 0.202 / 0.609 = 28.0 EUR/MWh
SRMC = 67.9 + 28.0 = 95.9 EUR/MWh

Worked example — MPP3 Coal (1,070 MW, η=0.46):
Coal price = 16.9 EUR/MWh, CO₂ = 84.6 EUR/tCO₂, eff_mult_coal = 1.006
Effective η = 0.46 × 1.006 = 0.463
Fuel cost = 16.9 / 0.463 = 36.5 EUR/MWh
Carbon cost = 84.6 × 0.341 / 0.463 = 62.3 EUR/MWh
SRMC = 36.5 + 62.3 = 98.8 EUR/MWh
(Coal is more expensive than gas per MWh because of high carbon costs despite cheap fuel — this is characteristic of the Dutch market with EU ETS prices above ~60 EUR/tCO₂)

2 9-Tranche Bid Spreading (Ward et al. 2019)

Real generators don't bid at exactly their SRMC. Some bid below (to ensure dispatch), others above (to capture scarcity rents). Ward et al. (2019) model this by splitting each generator's capacity into 9 tranches, each bidding at a different fraction of SRMC.

Tranche Spreading Formula
Per Generator: Split Capacity into 9 Tranches
For generator g with capacity C and SRMC S:

tranche_capacity(i) = C × w(i) ← w(i) are 9 calibrated widths, sum to 1.0
bid_price(i) = S + σ × α × a(i) × S ← a(i) are 9 adjustments, sorted ascending
= S × (1 + σ × α × a(i))

Parameters:
w(1)…w(9) = calibrated widths:
[0.297, 0.134, 0.121, 0.093, 0.109, 0.009, 0.006, 0.104, 0.126]
First tranche has ~30% of capacity (baseload), last two ~10% each (peaking)

a(1)…a(9) = calibrated adjustments (sorted):
[−0.521, −0.118, +0.066, +0.157, +0.484, +0.484, +0.889, +1.824, +1.824]
Negative → bid below SRMC (ensure dispatch); Positive → bid above (capture rents)

σ = 0.058 ← global spreading intensity (small: bids stay close to SRMC)
α = α_gas (0.903) for gas plants, α_coal (0.944) for coal, 0.5 for others

Effect: For a gas plant with SRMC = 96 EUR/MWh, σ=0.058, α=0.903:
Tranche 1 bid: 96 × (1 + 0.058 × 0.903 × (−0.521)) = 96 × 0.973 = 93.4
Tranche 5 bid: 96 × (1 + 0.058 × 0.903 × (+0.484)) = 96 × 1.025 = 98.4
Tranche 9 bid: 96 × (1 + 0.058 × 0.903 × (+1.824)) = 96 × 1.096 = 105.2
Range: 93.4 to 105.2 EUR/MWh — a ±6% spread around SRMC
Why is σ so small (0.058)? The calibrator found that minimal spreading works best for the Dutch market — most price variation is captured by the CHP must-run bands and the price adjustment layer rather than intra-generator bid spreading. The Ward et al. (2019) UK calibration had σ ≈ 1.0, but the Dutch market structure is different (dominated by gas + CHP rather than coal + nuclear).

3 CHP Must-Run: Multi-Band Bidding

Combined Heat and Power (CHP) plants produce electricity as a by-product of heat generation. When heat demand exists, they must run regardless of electricity price — so they bid at very low or negative prices to ensure dispatch. This is the single most important feature of the model.

CHP Must-Run Treatment
4-Band Bidding Structure
For each CHP generator with must-run capacity MR(t) (seasonal):

Band k: capacity = MR(t) × frac(k), bid = bid_price(k)

Calibrated band structure:
Band 1: frac = 0.008 (0.8%), bid = −254 EUR/MWh
Hard must-run: will pay up to 254 EUR/MWh to stay dispatched
Physical: industrial CHP with no ability to curtail (contractual/process heat)

Band 2: frac = 0.054 (5.4%), bid = −27 EUR/MWh
Moderate must-run: will accept moderate negative prices
Physical: district heating CHP with some thermal storage flexibility

Band 3: frac = 0.569 (56.9%), bid = +12 EUR/MWh
Flexible must-run: bids just above zero — dispatches in most hours
Physical: horticulture/greenhouse CHP with CO₂ and heat offtake

Band 4: frac = 0.369 (36.9%), bid = +72 EUR/MWh
Near-market: bids near gas SRMC — can curtail if prices too low
Physical: flexible industrial CHP that responds to spark spread

Remaining CHP capacity (1 − MR_fraction) gets normal 9-tranche treatment

Total CHP must-run volume (seasonal, sinusoidal):
~7,000 MW peak in January (high heat demand)
~2,000 MW trough in July (low heat demand)
This is ~40–70% of total residual demand in many hours
Why CHP dominates the model (+138% ΔRMSE when removed): The Netherlands has ~10 GW of decentral CHP (horticulture greenhouses, industrial sites, district heating) — one of the highest CHP penetrations in Europe. These plants bid at or below zero because their primary revenue comes from heat/CO₂ sales. Without this structural feature, the model cannot explain why Dutch prices go negative even when residual demand is positive, or why the supply curve has a large "flat" section at low prices.

4 Supply Curve Assembly & Market Clearing

Assembly
Sort All Bids → Clear Against Demand
Step 4a: Collect all bids
For 34 generators × up to 9 tranches each + CHP must-run bands:
→ Approximately 300–350 (bid_price, capacity_MW) pairs per hour

Step 4b: Sort by bid price ascending
CHP band 1 (−254) → CHP band 2 (−27) → CHP band 3 (+12) → ... → Coal (~99) → Gas CCGT (~96) → OCGT (~140) → ...

Step 4c: Compute cumulative capacity
Cum_Cap(i) = Σ capacity(1..i) ← running sum along sorted order

Step 4d: Find market-clearing price
P_base(t) = bid_price[ argmin(Cum_Cap(i) ≥ D_res(t)) ]
The bid price of the marginal generator (last one needed to meet demand)

Edge cases:
If D_res(t) ≤ 0: P = min(lowest_bid, 0) ← oversupply, negative price
If D_res(t) > total capacity: P = VOLL = 3,000 EUR/MWh ← scarcity cap

Optimisation: Supply curve only rebuilt when fuel prices or CHP volume changes
(fuel prices change daily, so ~365 curve builds per year, not 8,760).
Total runtime: ~50ms for 8,760 hours (NumPy vectorised search)
Worked example (winter hour, D_res = 12,280 MW):
CHP must-run (~6,800 MW) fills the bottom of the supply curve at −254 to +72 EUR/MWh.
Nuclear Borssele (485 MW) bids at ~12 EUR/MWh. Coal MPP3 tranches at ~94–105 EUR/MWh.
Gas CCGT tranches at ~93–106 EUR/MWh. After CHP + nuclear + some gas, cumulative capacity reaches 12,280 MW at a gas CCGT tranche bidding ~97 EUR/MWh. Base dispatch price = 97 EUR/MWh.

Stage 3: Price Adjustment Layer

10 additive correction terms applied post-dispatch, capturing effects beyond pure supply-demand intersection

The base dispatch price from the merit order captures the fundamental supply-demand balance, but real day-ahead prices are also influenced by strategic bidding, weather-driven demand shifts, wind curtailment effects, and scarcity dynamics that a static hourly merit order cannot fully represent. The price adjustment layer adds these effects as linear and quadratic corrections.

Complete Price Formula

Master Equation
Final Modelled Price P(t)
P(t) = P_base(t) ← from merit order clearing
+ γ_gas × (Gas(t) − mean(Gas)) ← [Term 1] gas sensitivity
+ γ_demand × (D_res(t) − mean(D_res)) / 1000 ← [Term 2] demand sensitivity
+ γ_intercept ← [Term 3] constant bias
+ γ_wind × (Wind(t) − mean(Wind)) / 1000 ← [Term 4] wind depression
+ γ_scarcity × [max(0, D_res(t) − Threshold) / 1000]² ← [Term 5] quadratic scarcity
+ γ_solar × (SolarRad(t) − mean(SolarRad)) / 100 ← [Term 6] rooftop PV proxy
+ γ_temp × (mean(Temp) − Temp(t)) ← [Term 7] temperature
+ γ_humidity × (Humidity(t) − mean(Humidity)) / 10 ← [Term 8] humidity
+ ε(t) ~ N(0, σ_noise²) ← [Term 9] smoothing noise

All deviation terms are centred on the annual mean — so the adjustment
is zero at the average condition and positive/negative at extremes.
Dividing by 1000 (demand, wind) or 100/10 (weather) normalises units to EUR/MWh.

Detailed Term-by-Term Explanation

Term 1
Gas Price Sensitivity — γ_gas = 1.94
Formula: +1.94 × (Gas(t) − 41.4)
Why needed: Gas sets the marginal price in ~60% of hours. The merit order already uses gas price in SRMC, but the market price responds even more strongly to gas price changes (strategic bidding, forward curve effects). This term captures that amplification.
Example: Gas at 55 EUR/MWh (13.6 above mean): adjustment = +1.94 × 13.6 = +26.4 EUR/MWh
Gas at 30 EUR/MWh (11.4 below mean): adjustment = +1.94 × (−11.4) = −22.1 EUR/MWh
Term 2
Demand Sensitivity — γ_demand = 1.78
Formula: +1.78 × (D_res(t) − 10,900) / 1000
Why needed: Captures startup costs, forward contracting effects, and price inertia that make prices respond more sharply to demand deviations than a step-function supply curve implies.
Example: D_res = 14,000 MW (3,100 above mean): adjustment = +1.78 × 3.1 = +5.5 EUR/MWh
D_res = 7,000 MW (3,900 below mean): adjustment = +1.78 × (−3.9) = −6.9 EUR/MWh
Term 3
Constant Intercept — γ_intercept = 2.39
Formula: +2.39 (constant, all hours)
Why needed: Corrects systematic level bias between merit-order clearing and actual day-ahead prices. The model's base dispatch tends to slightly under-predict average price levels due to unmodelled costs (grid fees, balancing, day-ahead auction dynamics).
Impact: Small constant shift: +2.39 EUR/MWh on every hour. Combined with the −6.21 EUR/MWh mean bias, this suggests the remaining gap comes from other systematic effects.
Term 4
Wind Depression — γ_wind = −11.18
Formula: −11.18 × (Wind(t) − 4,200) / 1000
Why needed: High wind generation displaces expensive gas plants from the merit order, depressing the marginal price beyond what the supply curve shift alone captures. The effect is stronger than just "less thermal needed" because wind also reduces scarcity rents and causes negative price episodes.
Example: Wind = 8,000 MW (3,800 above mean): adjustment = −11.18 × 3.8 = −42.5 EUR/MWh
Wind = 1,000 MW (3,200 below mean): adjustment = −11.18 × (−3.2) = +35.8 EUR/MWh
This is the 3rd most important individual parameter.
Term 5
Scarcity Adder — γ_scarcity = 5.54, threshold = 16,138 MW
Formula: +5.54 × [max(0, D_res(t) − 16,138) / 1000]²
Why needed: Prices rise superlinearly during scarcity events (high demand + low wind). The linear merit order cannot capture this — prices spike because generators exercise market power and scarcity rents emerge. The quadratic form captures this non-linearity.
Example: D_res = 18,000 MW → excess = (18,000 − 16,138)/1000 = 1.862 GW
Adjustment = 5.54 × 1.862² = 5.54 × 3.47 = +19.2 EUR/MWh
D_res = 20,000 MW → excess = 3.862 GW → adjustment = 5.54 × 14.92 = +82.7 EUR/MWh
Below 16,138 MW: this term contributes zero (max clamps to 0)
Term 6
Solar Radiation (Rooftop PV Proxy) — γ_solar = −16.09
Formula: −16.09 × (SolarRad(t) − 130) / 100
Why needed: The Netherlands has ~22 GW of rooftop solar PV that is not visible in the utility-scale solar data (~62 MW avg). Rooftop PV is already netted from the Load figure, but solar irradiance provides a direct proxy for how much rooftop PV is reducing demand in each hour. This is the strongest weather term.
Example: Bright summer noon, 600 W/m²: adjustment = −16.09 × (600−130)/100 = −16.09 × 4.7 = −75.6 EUR/MWh
Night (0 W/m²): adjustment = −16.09 × (0−130)/100 = −16.09 × (−1.3) = +20.9 EUR/MWh
2nd most important parameter group (weather: +28% ΔRMSE when removed).
Term 7
Temperature — γ_temp = −0.43
Formula: −0.43 × (11 − Temp(t)) = +0.43 × (Temp(t) − 11)
Why needed: Cold weather increases gas demand for space heating (NL has ~7 million gas-heated homes), creating competition for gas and potentially raising electricity prices. The negative sign is counterintuitive at first but is correct in the optimisation context: the temperature term interacts with other terms (especially solar and humidity) and this coefficient provides the best combined fit.
Example: Temp = −5°C: adjustment = −0.43 × (11 − (−5)) = −0.43 × 16 = −6.9 EUR/MWh
Impact: Small in magnitude — temperature effects are partially captured by the CHP seasonal profile and demand-based terms already.
Term 8
Humidity — γ_humidity = 4.07
Formula: +4.07 × (Humidity(t) − 80) / 10
Why needed: High humidity correlates with overcast, still weather — both wind and solar generation are low. This acts as a regime indicator for low-renewable conditions that push prices up because more thermal generation is needed.
Example: Humidity = 95%: adjustment = +4.07 × (95−80)/10 = 4.07 × 1.5 = +6.1 EUR/MWh
Humidity = 50%: adjustment = +4.07 × (50−80)/10 = 4.07 × (−3.0) = −12.2 EUR/MWh
(Low humidity = dry, clear, windy conditions → more renewables → lower prices)
Term 9
Gaussian Noise — σ_noise = 2.66
Formula: ε(t) ~ N(0, 2.66²) with fixed seed (42) for reproducibility
Why needed: The supply curve produces discrete price steps (jump when next generator's tranche is marginal). Real prices are smoother — this Gaussian noise softens the discrete steps into a more realistic continuous distribution. Also captures random factors (bilateral trading, forecast errors, balancing market spillover).
Magnitude: ±2.66 EUR/MWh (1 std dev) — small relative to the ~37 EUR/MWh RMSE, confirming this is a minor smoothing term, not a major model component.

Stage 4: Calibration Process

Two-stage optimisation: Bayesian exploration (500 trials) → local refinement (~3,300 evals). Total: ~185 seconds for 44 parameters.

1 Objective Function

The calibration minimises a weighted combination of 7 metrics, balancing overall accuracy (RMSE), tail behaviour, volatility matching, and distribution shape. This multi-objective approach prevents the optimiser from, e.g., getting a low RMSE by predicting a flat line near the mean.

Objective Function (minimise)
Weighted Multi-Metric Score
Obj = w₁ × RMSE ← w₁ = 1.0 Primary accuracy
+ w₂ × |P10_model − P10_actual| ← w₂ = 0.3 Low-price tail
+ w₃ × |P90_model − P90_actual| ← w₃ = 0.3 High-price tail
+ w₄ × |std_ratio − 1.0| × 50 ← w₄ = 0.5 Volatility (1.0 = perfect)
+ w₅ × |neg_freq_error| × 200 ← w₅ = 0.5 Negative price frequency
− w₆ × max(correlation, 0) × 10 ← w₆ = 0.2 Correlation bonus
− w₇ × max(duration_R², 0) × 20 ← w₇ = 0.3 Duration curve R² bonus

Why these specific metrics?
RMSE: punishes large errors heavily (squared), main accuracy measure
P10/P90: ensures tails (spikes and negatives) are captured, not just mean
std_ratio: prevents the model from being too flat or too volatile
neg_freq: Dutch market has ~3.6% negative prices — model must match this
correlation: rewards capturing hourly co-movement with actual prices
duration_R²: rewards matching the full price distribution shape

Best achieved objective: 31.66 (after SciPy Powell refinement)

2 Stage 1: Bayesian Optimisation (Optuna TPE)

Global Exploration
Tree-Structured Parzen Estimator — 500 Trials (~120s)
How TPE works:
1. Trial 1: Evaluate Ward et al. UK defaults (seeded starting point) — gives initial objective baseline
2. Trials 2–20: Random exploration of 44-dimensional parameter space (warm-up phase)
3. Trials 21–500: TPE builds two density models: p(θ | objective < threshold) and p(θ | objective ≥ threshold). It samples from the "good" distribution and evaluates. The surrogate model progressively focuses on promising regions.

Per trial:
→ Sample 44 parameters from TPE-guided distributions
→ Simulate 8,760 hourly prices (~50ms)
→ Compute 7 evaluation metrics
→ Calculate weighted objective
→ Update TPE surrogate model

Parameter constraints enforced during sampling:
• Tranche widths: sampled [0.01, 5.0], then normalised to sum to 1 (softmax-like)
• Tranche adjustments: sampled with overlapping ranges, then sorted ascending
• CHP band fractions: sampled [0.05, 5.0], normalised to sum to 1
• CHP band bids: sampled from overlapping ranges, sorted ascending
• Efficiency multipliers: bounded to physical ranges [0.92–1.08] for CCGT, etc.
• Scarcity: γ_scarcity ≥ 0 (prices only go up during scarcity, not down)

Output: Best parameter set found across 500 trials → passed to Stage 2

3 Stage 2: SciPy Powell Refinement

Local Refinement
Powell's Conjugate Direction Method — ~3,300 Evaluations (~65s)
Starting point: Best parameters from Bayesian stage

How Powell works:
1. Start with 44 coordinate directions (one per parameter)
2. Perform 1D line search along each direction → find minimum along that line
3. Construct new conjugate direction from the pattern of improvements
4. Replace the oldest direction and repeat
5. Continue until convergence (no direction yields significant improvement)

Why Powell over gradient-based methods?
• The objective function is not differentiable (discrete supply curve steps, argmin operations)
• Powell is gradient-free — only needs function evaluations
• Scales better than Nelder-Mead simplex in 44 dimensions (N line searches vs N+1 simplex vertices)

Monthly intercepts: Locked at 0 via bounds = (0.0, 0.0) for positions 38–48. This prevents overfitting to seasonal noise that would degrade OOS performance.

Parameter representation: Parameters converted to a flat 55-element array via to_array(). Tranche widths stored as log-space (inverse softmax), CHP fractions likewise. This ensures constraints (sum-to-1, positivity) are maintained during optimisation.

Output: Final 44 calibrated parameters → stored in calibration_results.json

4 Why Two Stages?

Bayesian alone is good at finding the right basin in 44-dimensional space but doesn't fine-tune within that basin (TPE samples discretely, not continuously).
Powell alone would get stuck in local minima because the 44-dimensional landscape has many flat ridges and shallow basins.
Together: TPE finds the right neighbourhood (global), Powell hones in on the precise minimum (local). Combined runtime ~185s for ~3,800 function evaluations. Each evaluation simulates a full year of hourly prices in ~50ms.

Enhanced Dispatch Model (Separate Validation)

Plant-level hourly dispatch for 26 named NL plants, validated against ENTSO-E generation-per-unit data

This is a separate model from the price model. It sits alongside the price model and uses some of the same calibrated parameters (efficiency multipliers), but it dispatches individual named plants — not technology aggregates — to check whether the implied merit ordering is physically reasonable.

Dispatch Algorithm (per hour)

Hourly Dispatch Loop
Enhanced Dispatch Model v2
Step 1: Compute available residual demand for named plants
D_named(t) = D_res(t) − CHP_decentral(t) − OTHER_MUSTRUN_MW
CHP_decentral: seasonal sinusoidal (~7 GW winter, ~2 GW summer)
OTHER_MUSTRUN_MW = 2,200 MW (biomass, waste, embedded generators)

Step 2: Compute effective SRMC for each of 26 named plants
SRMC_eff(p) = Fuel / (η(p) × eff_mult(p)) + CO₂ × CI / (η(p) × eff_mult(p))
+ CHP_offset(p, t) ← temperature-dependent for district heating
+ Startup_cost(p) ← 12 EUR/MWh large CCGT, 6 EUR/MWh small
+ VOM ← 2 EUR/MWh for gas and coal

Step 3: Sort plants by effective SRMC (merit order)

Step 4: Dispatch in merit order until D_named(t) is met
For each plant p in order:
available(p, t) = capacity(p) × monthly_availability(p, month(t))
× dispatch_cap_frac(p) ← for partial-load CHP plants
dispatch(p, t) = min(available(p, t), remaining_demand)
remaining_demand −= dispatch(p, t)

Special rules:
• Coal minimum run: once started, committed for 48 hours
• CHP heat profiles: 3 classes with different SRMC offsets:
- Industrial baseload: −25 to −45% of SRMC (always-on process heat)
- District heating: HDD-dependent offset (heating degree days from temperature)
- Industrial flex: −15% offset (moderate heat value)
• Monthly availability: 12-element array per plant from ENTSO-E data
(captures real maintenance outages, seasonal patterns)
